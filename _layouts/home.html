Brief Bio
I am a postdoc in Computational Pathology at Harvard Medical School. Previously I was a postdoctoral fellow in the Department of Statistics at the University of Washington supported by the NSF Mathematical Sciences Postdoctoral Research Fellowship. I received a PhD in statistics from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill where I was advised by Shankar Bhamidi and J.S Marron.

My research aims to solve challenging computational problems in biomedical sciences such as the analysis of very large scale medical images like stained whole slide images from Pathology. I’m broadly interested in developing statistical machine learning algorithms for data with a complex structure such as networks, images, and multi-view/modal data. The computational arm of my work weaves together statistics/probability, optimization, deep learning, and software engineering. The scientific arm of my work involves intensive collaboration with domain scientists/doctors and investigates basic scientific questions as well as clinical translational problems.

Computational pathology and precision medicine

Pathology lies at the heart of precision medicine; it is like the intellgience agency of medicine due to its central role in data collection and clinical decision making. Histological examination of a tissue biopsy has been the gold standard for cancer--and other disease--diagnosis for decades. New clinical measurement technologies (slide digitization, multiplex immunoflorecence, 3D histology, next-generation genomic sequencing, spatially resolved transcriptomics, microbiota characterization, etc) make it an exciting time for computational people to both impact clinical practice and help answer fundamental biology questions. My work in Pathology has two broad aims:
  • Use machine learning to augment clinical decision making for disease detection, diagnosis, prognosis, and response to treatment prediction.
  • Use computational/data driven approaches to discover new disease biomarkers and subtypes.
From a computational/statistical perspective, Pathology is a gold mine for interesting problems; you can find almost any data type e.g. networks, images, shapes, point clouds, etc. Almost everything is at play; there are issues of robustness, fairness, computation (datasets can easily be multiple terabytes), deep learning interpretability, high-dimensional inference, etc. Two of the most important technical challenges in computational pathology include:
  • Deep learning with massive and complex images; whole slide images can be 100,000 x 100,000 pixels--over 100,000 times more pixels than a typical ImageNet image--and the visual signals tend to be complex, subtle, and heterogenous.
  • Data integration for multiple data modalities such as images, molecular measurements, pathology reports, and clinical data.
